

## From Intuition to Formal Object: The Meaning-Formation of Scientific Concepts

The journey of a concept like "gene" from vague intuition to precise formal object follows a broadly recognizable trajectory, though never a perfectly linear one. Here are the major stages and the forces that drive transitions between them.

---

### Stage 1: Phenomenal Recognition (Pre-conceptual)

Before a concept has a name, scientists perceive a **regularity that demands explanation**. Mendel noticed that traits in peas followed stable ratios across generations. There was clearly *something* being transmitted — but it had no identity, no name, no ontological commitment. At this stage the "concept" is really just a gap in understanding with a felt shape: *something is going on here*.

**What drives the transition out:** The regularity proves robust and reproducible. It resists dismissal. Other researchers encounter the same pattern, creating social pressure to name and discuss it.

---

### Stage 2: Naming and Ostensive Definition

A term is coined to **point at the phenomenon** without yet committing to a mechanism. Johannsen coined *gene* (1909) deliberately as a theory-neutral placeholder — something that "accounts for hereditary traits," stripped of materialist assumptions. At this stage the concept is defined purely by what it *does* (its functional role in explanatory sentences), not by what it *is*.

The word functions almost indexically: "whatever it is that explains this pattern." This is productive vagueness — it lets a research community coordinate attention without premature closure.

**What drives the transition out:** Scientists need to *do things* with the concept — distinguish one gene from another, predict outcomes, design experiments. Mere ostension isn't enough for operational work.

---

### Stage 3: Operational / Relational Characterization

The concept gains precision through a web of **experimental operations and inferential relations**. For "gene," this meant: linkage mapping (Morgan), complementation tests, mutagenesis screens. Genes could now be individuated, ordered on chromosomes, and distinguished by function — all without anyone having seen one.

At this stage, the concept's meaning *is* the network of things you can do with it and infer from it. It's defined implicitly by its role in a system of practices and propositions, not by direct reference to a physical thing. This is where most scientific concepts do their heaviest explanatory work.

**What drives the transition out:** Persistent questions about mechanism ("but what *is* a gene, physically?") and the development of new instruments that make previously invisible substrates accessible.

---

### Stage 4: Material / Structural Identification

The concept gets **anchored to a physical substrate**. DNA is identified as the hereditary material (Avery 1944, Hershey–Chase 1952). The double helix is solved (1953). Suddenly "gene" has a candidate physical referent: a segment of DNA encoding a functional product.

This is often experienced as a dramatic clarification — the concept seems to "land" on something real. But it also introduces new tensions, because the material referent never perfectly matches the functional role that gave the concept its meaning in Stage 3.

**What drives the transition out:** Formalization pressure from theoretical frameworks (population genetics, information theory, molecular biology) that need concepts precise enough for mathematical manipulation.

---

### Stage 5: Formal / Mathematical Object

The concept becomes **precise enough to enter mathematical or computational frameworks**. In population genetics, a gene becomes an allele with a frequency in a population, subject to equations (Hardy-Weinberg, Fisher's fundamental theorem). In molecular biology, it becomes a sequence of nucleotides specifiable in a formal alphabet (A, T, G, C). In bioinformatics, it becomes an annotated region in a genome database with defined start/stop coordinates.

At this stage, the concept can be manipulated symbolically, computed over, and embedded in deductive systems. It achieves maximum precision — but also maximum abstraction from the original intuition.

**What drives the transition out (or back):** Empirical reality resists the clean formal picture.

---

### Stage 6: Fragmentation, Crisis, and Re-negotiation

Ironically, full formalization often **reveals that the concept doesn't carve nature at its joints** as cleanly as hoped. The modern "gene" is notoriously troubled: overlapping reading frames, alternative splicing, trans-splicing, regulatory elements far from coding regions, epigenetic inheritance, and non-coding functional RNAs all strain the classical definition. The concept fragments into sub-concepts (cistron, muton, recon; structural gene, regulatory gene; gene-P vs. gene-D in Moss's terminology).

This is not failure — it's the concept doing its job. The formal precision achieved in Stage 5 is exactly what makes it possible to *see* where the concept breaks down, which points toward deeper structure.

---

### What Drives Transitions Generally

| Driver | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Anomaly pressure** | Observations that the current conceptualization can't accommodate force refinement |
| **Instrumental access** | New technologies (microscopy, sequencing, crystallography) make previously invisible aspects of the referent visible, forcing reconceptualization |
| **Cross-domain import** | Borrowing formalisms from mathematics, information theory, or other sciences provides ready-made structures for sharpening concepts |
| **Pragmatic selection** | Concepts that enable prediction, manipulation, and coordination survive; those that don't get abandoned or absorbed |
| **Institutional standardization** | Textbooks, databases, nomenclature committees, and shared protocols force disambiguation and consensus |
| **Explanatory unification** | Pressure to connect disparate phenomena under a single framework drives concepts toward greater generality and formal precision |

---

### The Deep Pattern

The overall trajectory is from **indexical** (pointing at a phenomenon) to **functional** (defined by role in a practice) to **structural** (grounded in material substrate) to **formal** (entering mathematical/symbolic systems). But the process is always dialectical: formalization reveals inadequacies, which send the community back to re-examine the phenomenon, which generates new intuitions, which start the cycle again at a higher level of resolution.

What makes this process specifically *scientific* (as opposed to ordinary concept-formation) is the tight coupling between conceptual refinement and **empirical intervention** — the meaning of "gene" didn't sharpen through armchair analysis but through breeding experiments, X-ray diffraction, gel electrophoresis, and genome sequencing. The concept and the experimental practice co-evolved, each making the other more precise.
