

Here are three non-obvious structural parallels, stated as formal correspondences:

---

**1. Isostatic equilibrium ↔ Market equilibrium under exogenous shocks**

In isostasy, the lithosphere floats on a viscous asthenosphere. Adding mass (e.g., an ice sheet) depresses the crust; removing it triggers slow rebound. The restoring force is proportional to displacement, and the response is delayed by mantle viscosity. In a national economy, an exogenous shock (resource discovery, trade embargo) displaces prices and output from equilibrium; the system then adjusts back at a rate governed by institutional friction (regulatory lag, retraining time, capital reallocation speed).

**Formal correspondence:** Both are damped-response systems governed by the same differential structure — a restoring force proportional to displacement from equilibrium, retarded by a viscous medium. The mathematics is identical: exponential relaxation toward equilibrium with a time constant set by the ratio of the medium's resistance to the restoring force. This is not analogy; it is the same dynamical equation instantiated in different substrates.

---

**2. Subduction recycling ↔ Schumpeterian creative destruction as a conservation loop**

At convergent boundaries, oceanic crust is driven beneath continental crust into the mantle, destroyed as a surface structure, but its material re-enters the system — partially fueling arc volcanism and eventually new crust. Total lithospheric mass is conserved; only its organized surface form is rearranged. In an economy, creative destruction eliminates firms and industries, but their constituent resources (capital, labor, tacit knowledge) are released back into the economy's "mantle" and recombine into new enterprises.

**Formal correspondence:** Both are **closed-loop recycling systems operating on a conserved quantity** (mass / economic resources) where the destruction of organized surface structures is the *mechanism* by which the system renews its surface configuration. The invariant is the total stock; the variable is its structural arrangement. Crucially, in both, the recycling is not incidental — it is the *driver* of surface-level novelty. Block the subduction, and you halt volcanism; block firm exit, and you halt innovation. Destruction and creation are coupled as a single process, not sequential events.

---

**3. Wilson Cycle phase-locking ↔ Kondratieff long-wave economic cycles as forced oscillations of a dissipative system**

The Wilson Cycle describes the quasi-periodic assembly and breakup of supercontinents (~300–500 Myr). The cycle is not clockwork; it is driven by the interplay between heat accumulation beneath a supercontinent (insulating lid effect) and the resulting mantle upwelling that fragments it. The system oscillates because the state produced by each phase (assembly/dispersal) generates the boundary conditions that initiate the next phase. Kondratieff waves (~40–60 yr) in national economies follow the same logic: a phase of infrastructure investment creates overcapacity, which suppresses returns, which triggers restructuring, which eventually clears the way for the next investment wave.

**Formal correspondence:** Both are **endogenous oscillators** where the system's current macrostate generates the conditions that drive the transition to the opposing macrostate. The formal structure is a **relaxation oscillation** — slow accumulation of a driving variable (sub-continental heat / capital stock) up to a threshold, followed by rapid discharge (rifting / recession-restructuring), after which the cycle resets. The period is set not by an external clock but by the system's own dissipation rate. Both exhibit the same signature: irregular period, two distinct timescales (slow buildup, fast transition), and path-dependence in the details despite regularity in the gross pattern.
