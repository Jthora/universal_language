

## Five Levels of Abstraction

**Level 1 — Physical Chemistry**
Cellulose and lignin in dry wood undergo exothermic pyrolysis and combustion when their temperature exceeds ~300°C. The oxidation reactions release energy as heat and radiation, which conducts, convects, and radiates into adjacent dry biomass, raising it past the same thermal threshold. The chain continues as long as fuel and oxygen are available.

**Level 2 — Thermodynamics & Transport**
A local exothermic reaction elevates the temperature of a substrate past its activation energy barrier. The excess energy released propagates through the surrounding medium via thermal transport (conduction, convection, radiation), supplying neighboring regions with sufficient energy to overcome the same barrier. The process is self-sustaining wherever the medium's energy deficit (to reach threshold) is less than the energy flux from the active region.

**Level 3 — Dynamical Systems**
A bistable system (inert/burning) is coupled spatially: each element, once switched to the active state, emits a field (heat) that drives neighboring elements past a critical threshold into the same active state. The result is a propagating front — a traveling wave solution in a reaction-diffusion system — whose speed depends on the coupling strength and the gap between ambient state and threshold.

**Level 4 — Network Propagation**
A node in a network transitions irreversibly to an activated state when the input from its activated neighbors exceeds a local threshold. Each activated node becomes a source of the same input to its own neighbors. The cascade spreads through any connected region where the coupling exceeds the threshold deficit — a percolation process governed by connectivity and susceptibility.

**Level 5 — Abstract Principle**
*A local transition that produces the very conditions sufficient to trigger the same transition in coupled neighbors will propagate through any susceptible, connected medium.* Self-propagation arises whenever an event's output is the input its neighbors need to undergo the identical event. The only requirements are: (1) a threshold, (2) a transition that exceeds its own cost, and (3) coupling between adjacent elements.

---

The invariant across all five levels: **the effect of the event is itself the cause of the same event in what lies next to it.** Every level restates this — oxidation heating wood, energy exceeding a barrier, a bistable front advancing, a cascade crossing a network, or the bare recursive principle that output-as-input plus connectivity equals propagation.
