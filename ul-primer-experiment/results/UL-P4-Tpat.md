

Three non-obvious structural parallels, with formal correspondences:

---

**1. Mantle convection cells ↔ Capital circulation cycles**

In tectonics, mantle convection forms self-organizing cells: material rises at ridges driven by a thermal gradient, flows laterally, cools, and descends at subduction zones. In an economy, capital rises through investment into productive sectors, distributes laterally through supply chains and wages, encounters diminishing marginal returns (cools), and is consolidated/restructured through bankruptcy, acquisition, or savings drawdown.

**Formal correspondence:** Both are instances of dissipative structures maintained far from thermodynamic equilibrium by a throughput gradient (geothermal flux / aggregate demand injection). The number, scale, and geometry of circulation cells in both systems is governed by a dimensionless ratio structurally identical to the Rayleigh number: the ratio of driving force to the product of diffusive dissipation and viscous resistance. When this ratio crosses a critical threshold, symmetry-breaking occurs and organized flow cells emerge spontaneously. The governing equations in both cases are coupled nonlinear transport equations (momentum + scalar field), and the bifurcation structure — from quiescence to steady rolls to time-dependent turbulence — is the same.

---

**2. Elastic strain accumulation / seismic release ↔ Economic imbalance accumulation / crisis**

Tectonic plates are coupled at boundaries by friction. Slow relative motion loads elastic strain until a yield threshold is exceeded, producing an earthquake (stick-slip dynamics). Economies accumulate structural imbalances — debt overhang, asset bubbles, trade deficits — under steady growth, until a threshold triggers a rapid correction (financial crisis, recession).

**Formal correspondence:** Both are self-organized critical (SOC) systems. The defining formal features are shared: (a) slow, continuous loading coupled to fast, threshold-triggered discharge; (b) Gutenberg-Richter-type power-law frequency-magnitude distributions (earthquake magnitudes follow $\log N \sim a - bM$; economic crisis severities follow equivalent power-law tails); (c) stress transfer / contagion — a fault rupture redistributes stress to neighboring faults exactly as a bank failure redistributes counterparty risk through a network, both describable as percolation on a loaded graph; (d) aftershock sequences obeying Omori-law temporal decay, mirrored by cascading secondary failures after an economic shock. The systems share the same Bak-Tang-Wiesenfeld sandpile universality class. This is not analogy — the renormalization group structure that produces the critical exponents is identical.

---

**3. The Wilson Cycle ↔ Hegemonic long-wave cycles**

The Wilson Cycle: continents aggregate into a supercontinent, which then acts as a thermal insulator over the mantle, trapping heat, building sub-continental pressure, and eventually rifting the supercontinent apart — restarting the cycle. Hegemonic economic cycles: a dominant economy consolidates trade networks and institutional control, but dominance produces internal rigidities (rent-seeking, regulatory capture, infrastructure lock-in) that insulate it from competitive pressure, build internal inefficiency, and eventually fracture the hegemonic structure — redistributing productive capacity to peripheral economies.

**Formal correspondence:** Both are relaxation oscillators with the same phase-plane topology. A slow variable (sub-continental heat accumulation / institutional sclerosis, both monotonically increasing functions of the aggregated state's persistence) gradually shifts a bifurcation parameter until it crosses a threshold, triggering a fast variable (rifting / economic fragmentation). Formally, both follow a van der Pol-type dynamics: $\dot{x} = y - f(x)$, $\dot{y} = -\epsilon x$, where $x$ is the fragmentation state, $y$ is the accumulated stress, $f$ is a cubic nonlinearity encoding the bistability between aggregated and fragmented states, and $\epsilon \ll 1$ encodes the separation of timescales. The critical feature — that the product of aggregation is itself the cause of disaggregation — is not metaphorical but is the defining structural property of the nullcline geometry in both systems.
