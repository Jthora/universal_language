

## From Intuition to Formal Object: The Stages of Scientific Concept Formation

The trajectory of a concept like *gene* reveals a recurring pattern in science, one that the formalism you've presented actually metaphorizes in an interesting way:

### 1. Pre-formal Intuition (The Vague Field)

A pattern is noticed but unnamed. Mendel observed discrete inheritance ratios; breeders knew "like begets like." At this stage, the concept exists as a diffuse recognition — analogous to $\psi(x)$ as an unnormalized, spread-out wavefunction with no sharp features. There is signal, but no localization. The "belief field" $B^e$ is weak and broadly distributed.

**What drives transition:** Repeated empirical encounter. The pattern recurs across contexts enough that it demands a *name* — a cognitive handle that collapses the diffuse recognition into something referenceable.

### 2. Naming and Ostensive Fixing (Coarse Localization)

Johannsen coins *gene* (1909). The word doesn't yet have precise referent — it means roughly "the thing responsible for heritable unit-characters." This is the stage of ostensive definition: pointing at the phenomenon without yet specifying mechanism. The concept's "norm" $N(t) = \int_0^L |\psi|^2\, dx$ is now finite — it has been *bounded* — but the internal structure remains unresolved.

**What drives transition:** Theoretical pressure. Other concepts (chromosome, allele, locus) begin to constrain what *gene* can and cannot mean. The concept enters a relational web.

### 3. Relational Embedding (Spectral Decomposition)

The concept gains meaning by its *differential relations* to neighboring concepts. A gene is not a chromosome, not a trait, not an allele — it is defined partly by what it is not. This is where something like the spectral expansion $f_{\text{spec}}(x,t) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \left[\frac{a_n(t)}{n}\hat{\psi}_n + \frac{b_n(t)}{n^2}(ik_n)\hat{\psi}_n\right] e^{ik_n x}$ becomes apt as metaphor: the concept decomposes into harmonics of meaning, each mode $n$ corresponding to a distinct *use-context* (genetic mapping, biochemistry, population genetics, etc.). Higher-order modes ($1/n^2$ decay) contribute less — peripheral uses are weaker.

**What drives transition:** Formalization pressure from mathematics, experiment, and cross-disciplinary translation. The concept must become *operable* — you must be able to calculate with it.

### 4. Formal Crystallization (The Equation of Motion)

The concept becomes a defined term within a formal system. For *gene*, this arrives in stages: the classical gene (one gene–one enzyme, Beadle & Tatum), the molecular gene (DNA segment encoding a polypeptide, Watson & Crick), the modern gene (a transcription unit, with splicing, regulation, overlapping reading frames complicating the picture). At this stage, the concept obeys something like a governing equation — analogous to:

$$i\hbar\, D_t \psi = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} D_x^2 \psi + g_{\text{loc}}|\psi|^2\psi + g_{\text{glob}}N(t)\psi + \cdots$$

The concept now has *dynamics*: local self-interaction ($g_{\text{loc}}|\psi|^2\psi$ — the gene's meaning feeds back on itself), global coupling ($g_{\text{glob}}N(t)\psi$ — the total state of the field constrains each part), and gauge structure ($D_x = \partial_x - i(e/\hbar)A$ — the concept's meaning is frame-dependent, shifting under theoretical "gauge transformations" like the shift from Mendelian to molecular frameworks).

**What drives transition:** Anomalies. The formal object encounters phenomena it cannot accommodate cleanly.

### 5. Crisis and Re-opening (Dissipation and Renewal)

The term $-i\gamma(\sigma, \beta)\psi$ in your formalism — a dissipative, damping term — captures this stage. The concept *loses coherence*. For *gene*, this is the contemporary situation: epigenetics, non-coding RNA, horizontal gene transfer, and ENCODE's findings about pervasive transcription all erode the sharp boundaries of "gene." The concept partially de-formalizes, returning to a state of productive vagueness — but now at a higher level of sophistication.

### What Drives Transitions Generally?

Three forces:

1. **Empirical friction** — data that resists current categorization
2. **Inferential integration** — the need for concepts to cohere with each other within theoretical networks
3. **Operational demand** — the requirement that concepts be *usable* in calculation, prediction, and experimental design

The process is not linear. Concepts oscillate between precision and vagueness as the field evolves — each return to vagueness is more structured than the last, carrying the residue of prior formalizations. The meaning of *gene* in 2026 is vague in a fundamentally different way than it was in 1905: it is vague *against the background* of everything molecular biology has formalized and then problematized.

This is the deep pattern: **scientific concept formation is not convergence toward a fixed point, but a spiral of successive localizations and de-localizations in meaning-space**, each pass resolving finer structure while revealing new ambiguity at smaller scales.
