

# Recursive Decomposition of Democracy

## Level 1 — Institutional Machinery (most concrete)

**Components:** constitutions, legislatures, courts, elections, political parties, bureaucracies, free press, civil society organizations

**Composition → Level 2:** These institutions are *implementations* of a set of procedural principles. A legislature instantiates representation; an election instantiates consent; a court instantiates rule of law; a free press instantiates accountability. Each institution exists to give concrete, enforceable form to an abstract procedural norm.

---

## Level 2 — Procedural Principles

**Components:** majority rule, minority protection, periodic free elections, separation of powers, due process, transparency, peaceful transfer of power, constitutional supremacy

**Composition → Level 1:** These principles are the "operating rules" that, when codified and staffed, *produce* the institutions above. Majority rule + periodic elections → legislature. Separation of powers + due process → independent judiciary. Transparency + free expression → free press and civil society oversight.

**Composition → Level 3:** Each procedural principle is itself justified by a deeper normative commitment. Majority rule operationalizes *political equality*. Due process operationalizes *individual rights*. Transparency operationalizes *accountability*. The procedures are chosen precisely because they are the best-known mechanisms for honoring these norms simultaneously.

---

## Level 3 — Normative Commitments

**Components:** political equality, individual liberty, collective self-governance (popular sovereignty), rule of law, pluralism, accountability

**Composition → Level 2:** These values *select and constrain* the procedures. Political equality demands one-person-one-vote. Liberty demands rights-protected zones against majority overreach. Popular sovereignty demands that ultimate authority traces back to the people. Pluralism demands space for dissent. Together they generate the specific procedural bundle at Level 2 — and rule out alternatives (e.g., a benevolent dictator satisfies none of these jointly).

**Composition → Level 4:** Each normative commitment rests on a still-deeper claim about what persons *are* and what they are *owed*. Political equality derives from moral equality of persons. Liberty derives from rational agency. Collective self-governance derives from the idea that legitimate authority requires the consent of those it binds. These are anthropological and moral premises.

---

## Level 4 — Moral-Anthropological Premises

**Components:** moral equality of persons, rational agency (capacity for reason and reflection), fallibility (no person or group is epistemically infallible), dignity (intrinsic worth independent of social role), capacity for cooperation under disagreement

**Composition → Level 3:** Moral equality + rational agency → political equality (if all are equally capable of moral reasoning, none has a natural title to rule). Rational agency + dignity → individual liberty (agents with intrinsic worth must not be merely instrumentalized). Fallibility → pluralism and accountability (because no one is infallible, power must be checked and dissent tolerated). Capacity for cooperation under disagreement → collective self-governance (it is *possible* for people who disagree to nonetheless author shared rules).

**Composition → Level 5:** These premises are themselves instantiations of the most abstract philosophical primitives — the bedrock categories out of which any political theory must be built.

---

## Level 5 — Philosophical Primitives (most abstract)

**Components:**

| Primitive | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Agency** | The capacity of an entity to act intentionally in the world |
| **Equality** | The relation of non-hierarchy between members of a class |
| **Legitimacy** | The property that distinguishes binding authority from mere coercion |
| **Finitude** | The bounded, error-prone nature of any knower or actor |
| **Intersubjectivity** | The irreducibly social structure of meaning, recognition, and normativity |

**Composition → Level 4:**

- **Agency** → rational agency, dignity (an agent is a locus of intention and therefore of worth)
- **Equality** → moral equality of persons (agency is universally distributed, so the default relation is non-hierarchy)
- **Legitimacy** → the demand for consent (authority that cannot be justified to those it binds is mere force)
- **Finitude** → fallibility (no agent has total knowledge; error is structural, not accidental)
- **Intersubjectivity** → capacity for cooperation under disagreement (meaning and norms are co-constituted, so governance is necessarily a *shared* project)

---

## The Full Composition Chain

```
Level 5  Agency + Equality + Legitimacy + Finitude + Intersubjectivity
           │
           ▼  instantiate as claims about persons
Level 4  Moral equality, Rational agency, Dignity, Fallibility, Cooperative capacity
           │
           ▼  generate value commitments
Level 3  Political equality, Liberty, Popular sovereignty, Rule of law, Pluralism, Accountability
           │
           ▼  select & constrain operating rules
Level 2  Majority rule, Minority protection, Free elections, Separation of powers, Due process, Transparency
           │
           ▼  are codified & staffed as
Level 1  Constitutions, Legislatures, Courts, Elections, Parties, Press, Civil society
```

**Key insight:** Democracy is not a single idea but a *compositional stack*. Each level is necessary but insufficient alone — institutions without normative grounding are hollow; philosophical primitives without institutional incarnation are inert. The coherence of democracy lies in the *vertical alignment* across all five levels: every institution should be traceable, through procedures and values, down to the primitives, and every primitive should have an institutional expression pointing back up.
