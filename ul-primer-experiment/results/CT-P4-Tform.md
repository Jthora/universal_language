

The formation of a precise scientific concept from a vague intuition typically proceeds through several recognizable stages, each driven by distinct pressures:

## 1. Pre-conceptual intuition
A pattern is noticed but not yet named. Mendel observes that traits segregate in regular ratios; early thermodynamicists sense that heat engines have some fundamental limit. At this stage, the "concept" lives as a cluster of empirical regularities and analogies. What drives departure from this stage is **persistent explanatory need** — the pattern keeps appearing, and existing vocabulary cannot capture it.

## 2. Naming and ostensive definition
The intuition receives a label — "gene," "entropy," "field" — but the label is defined mostly by pointing at instances rather than by formal criteria. The word functions as a placeholder that coordinates discourse: researchers can now refer to the same vague thing. The transition out of this stage is driven by **disambiguation pressure**. Different workers use the term in subtly incompatible ways, and contradictions or failed predictions force clarification of what the term must and must not cover.

## 3. Operational specification
The concept gets tied to measurable procedures. A gene becomes "that which segregates in a cross and maps to a locus"; entropy becomes "the integral of δQ/T along a reversible path." This stage constrains the concept's extension, but the definition is still anchored to particular experimental setups. The transition forward is driven by **generalization demands** — new domains (statistical mechanics, molecular biology) require the concept to apply in contexts where the original operations don't directly reach.

## 4. Formal-structural definition
The concept is re-grounded in a mathematical or logical structure that abstracts away from any particular measurement. Entropy becomes $S = -k_B \sum_i p_i \ln p_i$, defined over probability distributions on microstates. A gene becomes a sequence of nucleotides encoding a functional product. At this stage the concept is embedded in a network of formal relations — partition functions, Legendre transforms, critical exponents — and its meaning is largely constituted by its position in that network, much as the passage above defines thermodynamic quantities through their structural interrelations.

## 5. Universality and abstraction
The formalized concept reveals that superficially different phenomena share the same deep structure. The renormalization group shows that critical exponents are universal across wildly different physical systems; information-theoretic entropy unifies thermodynamic entropy with communication theory. The concept has now transcended its origin and become a piece of transferable formal machinery.

## What drives the transitions

Three recurring forces push concepts along this trajectory:

- **Empirical friction.** Vague concepts generate ambiguous predictions. Failed or contradictory experiments force tighter specification.
- **Inter-theoretic contact.** When a concept must function across domains (thermodynamics ↔ statistical mechanics, classical genetics ↔ molecular biology), its definition must be abstracted from domain-specific particulars. This is analogous to how fixed points of the RG flow are domain-independent — they capture what is invariant across different "scales" of description.
- **Mathematical consolidation.** Formal frameworks reveal hidden constraints and connections. Once entropy is written as a function of microstate probabilities, the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and the Sackur-Tetrode equation become derivable consequences rather than separate empirical facts. The formalism itself generates new meaning by exposing structural relationships that were invisible at the intuitive stage.

The process is not strictly linear — concepts can be driven back to earlier stages by revolutionary discoveries (the gene concept was substantially destabilized by introns, epigenetics, and horizontal transfer). But the general arc from ostension to operational definition to structural embedding to universal abstraction is remarkably consistent across the sciences.
