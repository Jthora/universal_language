

**Level 1 — Physical Chemistry**
Cellulose and lignin in dry wood undergo exothermic pyrolysis and combustion when their temperature exceeds ~300°C (flash point). This releases energy primarily as infrared radiation and convective hot gases, which raise the temperature of adjacent dry lignocellulosic material past the same threshold, initiating the same radical chain reactions there. The fire spreads because each local combustion event outputs enough thermal energy to push neighboring fuel past its activation energy barrier.

**Level 2 — Thermodynamics / Heat Transfer**
A burning region is a high-temperature source that transfers heat to its cooler surroundings via radiation, convection, and conduction. When a neighboring fuel element absorbs enough energy to cross a critical temperature threshold, it transitions irreversibly from an unburning to a burning state, becoming a new heat source. The fire spreads because each newly burning element generates sufficient excess energy to drive the same phase transition in adjacent elements.

**Level 3 — Dynamical Systems / Reaction–Diffusion**
The system can be modeled as a spatial field with a local state variable (temperature/burning status) governed by a reaction–diffusion equation. Each point has a stable "cold" equilibrium and an unstable threshold above which positive feedback (exothermic reaction) drives it to a "burning" state. Diffusion of the activating quantity (heat) into neighboring regions pushes them past the same threshold. The fire is a propagating front — a traveling-wave solution sustained because the reaction at the front generates enough diffusible activator to destabilize the next layer.

**Level 4 — Cascade / Network Propagation**
An agent in a susceptible state becomes active when the influence it receives from active neighbors exceeds a threshold. Once active, it exerts that same kind of influence on its own neighbors. The fire is a contagion cascade: each activation event is both a consequence of prior activations and a cause of subsequent ones. Spread occurs because the output of each local activation is sufficient to trigger activation in at least one connected susceptible neighbor (the effective reproduction number $R > 1$).

**Level 5 — Abstract Principle**
A local transition that produces enough of its own triggering condition to induce the same transition in adjacent susceptible elements will propagate. Spread is the inevitable consequence of any process whose output is a sufficient cause of its own recurrence in a connected medium.
