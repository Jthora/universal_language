## UL-P1 (Topological-Harmonic) — 7 trials

**Overall:** The weakest primer. Claude frequently dismisses or partially dismisses the formalism, particularly the invented terms ("vrîtha," "7-fold harmonic lattice").

| Trial | Key Observations |
|-------|-----------------|
| UL-P1-T1 | Critiques the primer: identifies real vs. fictitious notation, then answers independently. "That gap — between syntactic plausibility and semantic constraint — is where most confusion... actually lives." Insightful, but primer isn't scaffolding the reasoning. |
| UL-P1-T2 | **Explicitly dismisses primer**: "I won't treat it as a meaningful formalism, but the actual question is interesting." Then gives standard quantum cognition analysis. |
| UL-P1-T5 | **Ignores primer entirely**: "The mathematical formalism you've provided doesn't connect to the question, so I'll address it directly." Gives clean info-theory curriculum design. |
| UL-P1-Tpat | Strong despite primer skepticism. 3 formal parallels (convection↔capital, Wilson cycle↔Kondratiev, transform faults↔trade corridors) with SOC equations. TES=5. |
| UL-P1-Thier | 5-level decomposition, Level 1 down to {Selfhood, Plurality, Finitude, Normativity}. Includes formal composition expression. TES=4. |
| UL-P1-Tabs | Standard 5 levels, good invariant identification. Not distinguishable from NL. TES=4. |
| UL-P1-Tform | **Best P1 output**: Maps concept formation onto the primer's equation terms (symmetry-breaking for naming, harmonic forcing for relational structuring, variational structure for formalization). TES=5. |

**Lesson learned:** Including "invented" terminology (vrîtha) triggers Claude's skepticism and causes it to disengage from the formalism. The other primers avoid this mistake.

| Trial | Key Observations |

| UL-P2-T1 | Deep engagement: interprets Φ ≅ F(Φ) as the common root of language (recursive grammars), consciousness (strange loops), and math (Gödel's fixed-point lemma). "That invariance *is* the relationship." |

| UL-P2-Tpat | 3 parallels framed through UL-P2 lens: convection↔capital (nonlinear self-interaction), ridge/subduction↔creative destruction (fixed-point recycling topology, Φ ≅ F(Φ)), earthquake↔boom-bust (relaxation oscillator). TES=5. |
| UL-P2-Thier | **Standout output**: Democracy as categorical fixed point. Φ_democracy ≅ F(Φ_democracy) : colim[F⁰(∅) → F¹(∅) → ... → F⁴(∅)]. Level 5 primitives = {Agency, Plurality, Normativity, Mutual Recognition}. Explicit formula. TES=5. |

## NAV-P (Naive Prompt Engineering) — 3 trials

**Overall:** Surprisingly the strongest condition in the experiment. The model, when simply told to "think structurally across domains," produces its best work.

| Trial | Observations |
|-------|-------------|
| NAV-P-T1 | **Best T1 in the experiment.** 8-section presheaf-theoretic framework. Formal table comparing Math/Language/Consciousness across 5 features. Gödel/Tarski connection to hard problem. Generative predictions. Three pages of dense, well-structured analysis. |
| NAV-P-T2 | **Among the best T2 outputs.** 6 QM-belief correspondences: superposition→pre-commitment, entanglement→worldview coherence (with Bell's theorem analogy), measurement→belief attribution (relational interpretation), decoherence→social stabilization, uncertainty→conviction/openness complementarity. Phase transition prediction for belief systems. |
| NAV-P-T5 | **Longest and most detailed T5.** Rate-distortion theory for assessment, Kolmogorov complexity for curriculum metrics, phase transitions in learning (percolation threshold), specific testable predictions about concept clustering. Goes far beyond any other T5 output. |

**Why NAV-P works:** The naive prompt provides explicit metacognitive instruction that activates the model's already-strong cross-domain reasoning capabilities. For a frontier model, *being asked to think structurally* is sufficient — formal mathematical scaffolding adds nothing.
