## PART I: THE PRIM HOM-SET ENUMERATION (Gap B-4)

### 1.1 Setup

Let **Prim** be the full subcategory of Lang(Σ_UL) whose objects are the five simplest algebras — each generated by a single geometric primitive:

| Object | Generator | Carrier content |
|---|---|---|
| **Pt** | A single point $p$ | $(\text{Pt})_e = \{p\}$, all other sorts contain only what the operations produce from $p$ |
| **Ln** | A single directed line segment $\ell$ (= two points + direction) | $(\text{Ln})_e = \{p_1, p_2\}$, $(\text{Ln})_r = \{\ell\}$, plus derived elements |
| **An** | A single angle $\theta$ (= two rays from a common vertex) | $(\text{An})_e = \{v, p_1, p_2\}$, $(\text{An})_r = \{\ell_1, \ell_2\}$, $(\text{An})_m = \{\theta\}$, plus derived elements |
| **Cv** | A single smooth curve $c$ | $(\text{Cv})_e = \{p_{\text{start}}, p_{\text{end}}\} \cup \text{support}$, $(\text{Cv})_r = \{c\}$, plus derived |
| **En** | A single closed enclosure $\varepsilon$ | $(\text{En})_e$ contains interior and boundary points, $(\text{En})_a$ includes the enclosed region |

These are not arbitrary — each is the **free algebra on a single generator of the corresponding geometric type**, closed under the Σ_UL operations. They represent the minimal language capable of expressing one primitive.

### 1.2 What the Hom-Sets Mean

A Σ_UL-homomorphism $\phi: P \to Q$ between two primitive algebras maps the generator of $P$ to an element of $Q$ of the appropriate sort, preserving all operations. Since each algebra is generated by a single primitive, a homomorphism is determined by where the generator goes — but it must land on an element that satisfies all the structural constraints of the source.

**Key principle:** $\text{Hom}(P, Q) \neq \emptyset$ if and only if $Q$ contains a sub-construction that has the same structural profile as $P$'s generator.

### 1.3 Enumeration of All 25 Hom-Sets

We now compute $\text{Hom}(P, Q)$ for all pairs. We write $|H|$ for cardinality (or describe the structure when infinite).

---

#### (1) $\textbf{Hom(Pt, Pt)}$

A morphism maps the point $p$ to a point. Pt has one point. One choice.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Pt}, \text{Pt})| = 1$$

This is the identity morphism. **Aut(Pt) = {id}** — a bare point has no nontrivial automorphism.

#### (2) $\textbf{Hom(Pt, Ln)}$

A morphism maps the point $p$ to a point in Ln. Ln has two points ($p_1, p_2$). Two choices.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Pt}, \text{Ln})| = 2$$

#### (3) $\textbf{Hom(Pt, An)}$

An has three points ($v, p_1, p_2$) — the vertex and the two ray endpoints.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Pt}, \text{An})| = 3$$

#### (4) $\textbf{Hom(Pt, Cv)}$

Cv has at least two distinguished points (start, end), plus the points along the curve's support. In the free algebra, the curve is a single smooth arc: the start and end are the two distinguished entity-sort elements. (Points along the interior are not individually named in the algebra unless explicitly constructed.)

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Pt}, \text{Cv})| = 2$$

(Mapping $p$ to either the curve's start or end point.)

#### (5) $\textbf{Hom(Pt, En)}$

En's entity-sort contains the boundary points and the enclosed region's representative point(s). In the minimal free algebra, the enclosure is defined by its boundary (a single closed curve). The distinguished entity-sort elements are: the boundary curve's start/end (which coincide, so 1 point on the boundary) plus an interior representative. However, an enclosure is characterized not by individual boundary points but by the boundary as a whole.

In the free construction, the enclosure $\varepsilon$ is built from a closed curve. The entity sort contains: the curve's starting (=ending) point, and the embedded entity $\text{embed}^{-1}(\varepsilon)$ if we think of the enclosure as "nominalized." More carefully: the free algebra generated by a single enclosure has at least one distinguished entity — the point that anchors the enclosure's construction.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Pt}, \text{En})| = 1$$

(The one anchoring point of the enclosure construction. Alternative boundary points are not individually distinguished in the free algebra; the boundary is a single named curve, not a point set.)

---

#### (6) $\textbf{Hom(Ln, Pt)}$

A morphism maps the line $\ell$, with its two endpoint entities $p_1, p_2$ and the relation $\ell \in (\text{Ln})_r$, into Pt. It must map $p_1, p_2 \in (\text{Ln})_e$ to elements of $(\text{Pt})_e = \{p\}$: both go to $p$. It must map $\ell \in (\text{Ln})_r$ to an element of $(\text{Pt})_r$. But $\text{Pt}_r$ consists only of elements derivable from a single point $p$. There is no relation constructible from a single point alone — the `compose` operation requires two existing relations, `invert` requires one existing relation, and `predicate` produces an assertion, not a relation. The modifier sort $\text{Pt}_m$ is similarly empty beyond what `abstract(p)` generates.

Actually: can we get a relation from a single point? We need some $r \in G_r$ that is derivable from $p$ alone. Looking at the operations: `compose(r₁, r₂)` requires existing relations; `invert(r)` requires an existing relation; no operation produces a relation from entities alone except `predicate(e₁, r, e₂)` which requires a relation as input. This is circular. **There is no way to derive a relation from entities alone.**

$$\text{Hom}(\text{Ln}, \text{Pt}) = \emptyset$$

A point algebra has no relations. A line algebra requires a relation. No homomorphism exists.

#### (7) $\textbf{Hom(Ln, Ln)}$

The line has two points and one directed relation. A homomorphism maps each to the corresponding sort in the target Ln. The two points can map to $\{p_1, p_2\}$ in two ways (identity and swap). If we swap the points, the relation $\ell$ (directed from $p_1$ to $p_2$) must map to $\text{invert}(\ell)$ (directed from $p_2$ to $p_1$) to preserve the predicate structure. This is valid: $\text{invert}$ is a Σ_UL operation and inversion of a line segment is well-defined.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Ln}, \text{Ln})| = 2$$

The two morphisms are: identity, and the endpoint-swap (= direction reversal). **Aut(Ln) = ℤ/2ℤ** — a line segment has a reflection symmetry.

#### (8) $\textbf{Hom(Ln, An)}$

An has two rays ($\ell_1, \ell_2$) meeting at vertex $v$, with endpoints $p_1, p_2$. The relation sort of An contains $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ (and their inversions and compositions). A line algebra's generator $\ell$ can map to either $\ell_1$ or $\ell_2$ (or their inversions). For each choice, the two endpoints map accordingly.

- $\ell \mapsto \ell_1$: then $p_1 \mapsto v$, $p_2 \mapsto p_1$ (the vertex and far endpoint of ray 1)
- $\ell \mapsto \text{invert}(\ell_1)$: then $p_1 \mapsto p_1$, $p_2 \mapsto v$
- $\ell \mapsto \ell_2$: then $p_1 \mapsto v$, $p_2 \mapsto p_2$
- $\ell \mapsto \text{invert}(\ell_2)$: then $p_1 \mapsto p_2$, $p_2 \mapsto v$

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Ln}, \text{An})| = 4$$

#### (9) $\textbf{Hom(Ln, Cv)}$

A curve has a start and end point and a relation (the curve itself, viewed as a directed connection). The curve's direction can be preserved or inverted.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Ln}, \text{Cv})| = 2$$

(The line embeds as the curve "straightened" — or reversed. Two standard morphisms.)

#### (10) $\textbf{Hom(Ln, En)}$

The enclosure is built from a closed curve — start = end. The entity sort has essentially one distinguished point. The relation sort contains the boundary curve. A line has two distinct endpoints; mapping both to the same point collapses the distinction. The homomorphism maps $\ell$ to the boundary curve (or its inverse). Both endpoints map to the single anchor point. This is valid: $p_1 \mapsto p_{\text{anchor}}$, $p_2 \mapsto p_{\text{anchor}}$. But wait — does the operation `predicate(p_1, ℓ, p_2)` map correctly? It maps to `predicate(p_anchor, boundary, p_anchor)` — a self-loop assertion. This is well-defined in G.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Ln}, \text{En})| = 2$$

(The boundary curve or its inverse; both endpoints collapse to the anchor.)

---

#### (11) $\textbf{Hom(An, Pt)} = \emptyset$

An requires relations and modifiers. Pt has neither. No homomorphism exists (same argument as Hom(Ln, Pt)).

#### (12) $\textbf{Hom(An, Ln)}$

An angle has two rays and a modifier (the angle value $\theta$). A homomorphism must send both rays to relations in Ln and the angle-modifier to a modifier in Ln. Ln has one relation $\ell$ (and $\text{invert}(\ell)$). The two rays must map to $\{\ell, \text{invert}(\ell)\}$. The modifier $\theta$ must map to the modifier derivable from $\ell$ in Ln.

What modifiers exist in Ln? From a single line segment, `abstract(p₁)` gives a modifier based on point 1, `abstract(p₂)` gives another. But the angle modifier $\theta$ characterizes the angular relationship between two rays — a property that requires two independent directions. In a single-line algebra, there is only one direction (and its inverse). The angle between a line and its inverse is always $\pi$ (or 0 if they are the same). So the only angle constructible in Ln is the trivial one.

A morphism would force $\theta \mapsto$ the unique angle in Ln (= $\pi$ or $0$). This IS a valid homomorphism if the source angle is $\pi$ or $0$. But for a general angle generator (with $\theta \neq 0, \pi$), this collapses the angular information. The free algebra An is generated by a *generic* angle — not a specific value. The question is whether there is a homomorphism that maps the generic angle to the degenerate one.

In the free algebra setting: the generator $\theta$ is a formal modifier with no imposed relations except those following from the Σ_UL axioms. Mapping it to any modifier in Ln is valid as a set function, but it must preserve all operation equations. The key equation is: $\theta$ was defined as the angle between $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$, and in An this is a primitive (no equation forces it to equal anything). In Ln, with $\ell_1 = \ell$ and $\ell_2 = \text{invert}(\ell)$, the angle between them is $\pi$. So the homomorphism sends the generic angle $\theta$ to $\pi$.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{Ln})| = 1$$

(The degenerate angle: both rays collapse onto one line, angle becomes $\pi$.)

#### (13) $\textbf{Hom(An, An)}$

The generic angle has vertex $v$ and two rays. An automorphism can swap the two rays (reflecting the angle) or keep them. Swapping the rays maps $\theta$ to $-\theta$ (or equivalently $2\pi - \theta$). The identity preserves $\theta$. So:

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{An})| = 2$$

**Aut(An) = ℤ/2ℤ** — reflection symmetry.

#### (14) $\textbf{Hom(An, Cv)}$

A curve can exhibit only one tangent angle at each point. Two rays meeting at a vertex can be realized as the tangent directions at a curve's endpoint (if the curve has a corner) or as two separate curves. In the free algebra of Cv (a single smooth curve), the start point has one tangent direction and the end point has one tangent direction. These provide two rays meeting at... they don't meet at a common vertex. 

However, if the curve is closed ($p_{\text{start}} = p_{\text{end}}$), the start/end tangent directions give an angle at the closing point. But Cv is generated by a generic (open) curve.

For an open curve: the start and end tangent directions don't share a vertex. There's no angle constructible from a single open curve in the free algebra. The rays of An must map to relations in Cv that share an endpoint, and the only way two directed segments share a starting point is through `compose` — but that produces a single composed relation, not two separate rays.

$$\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{Cv}) = \emptyset$$

(A single open curve does not contain an angle sub-construction. The angle requires two rays from a common vertex; an open curve provides only one continuous path.)

**Remark.** If Cv were generated by a *closed* curve, an angle would exist at the closing point. But the generic curve in the free algebra is open.

#### (15) $\textbf{Hom(An, En)}$

An enclosure is built from a closed curve — hence the boundary's start/end tangent directions at the closing point could define an angle. For a smooth closed curve, the tangent is continuous at the closing point, giving an angle of 0 (the tangent doesn't jump). This is a degenerate angle.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{En})| = 1$$

(The angle maps to the degenerate angle 0 at the enclosure's smooth closing point.)

#### (16) $\textbf{Hom(Cv, Pt)} = \emptyset$

Same argument as Hom(Ln, Pt): a point generates no relations.

#### (17) $\textbf{Hom(Cv, Ln)}$

A curve is a "varying-direction line." A morphism Cv → Ln must map the curve to a straight-line relation. This forgets the curvature — a valid homomorphism if it preserves the endpoints and the direction relationship. The curve's basic structure includes (start, end, curve-relation). Mapping to (p_1, p_2, ℓ) or (p_2, p_1, invert(ℓ)):

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Cv}, \text{Ln})| = 2$$

(The curve's relational skeleton maps to the line, with curvature information lost. Two choices: preserve or reverse direction.)

#### (18) $\textbf{Hom(Cv, An)} = \emptyset$

An angle's relation sort contains two rays meeting at a point. A curve has a single directed path. To map the curve to An, the curve relation would need to map to one of the two rays — but then the curve's other structural features (curvature, the fact that it's a continuously varying direction) have no target. More precisely: Cv has a single generator in the relation sort ($c$); An has two generators in the relation sort ($\ell_1, \ell_2$). A homomorphism from Cv to An maps $c$ to a relation in An — it can go to $\ell_1$, $\ell_2$, their inversions, or their compositions. Mapping $c \mapsto \ell_1$ is valid: start of $c$ maps to vertex $v$, end of $c$ maps to $p_1$. The curvature of $c$ (a modifier-sort datum) must map to a modifier in An — it maps to the angle $\theta$ or a derived modifier. This is structurally valid.

Wait — re-examining. The curve $c$ is a single directed connection in Cv's relation sort. An angle algebra's relation sort includes $\ell_1, \ell_2, \text{compose}(\ell_1, \ell_2), \text{invert}(\ell_1), \ldots$ We need $c$ to map to one of these. If $c \mapsto \ell_1$: start maps to $v$, end maps to $p_1$. The reverse of $c$ maps to $\text{invert}(\ell_1)$. The curvature $\kappa$ of $c$ (encoded in the modifier sort) maps to... what modifier in An? The abstract of $v$ or $p_1$, or the angle $\theta$ itself. If $\kappa \mapsto \theta$: this is a valid assignment provided the operation equations are preserved. The key question: does any operation equation in Cv force a constraint that can't be satisfied in An? In the free algebra setting, the curve's curvature is a free modifier — so $\kappa \mapsto \theta$ is valid.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Cv}, \text{An})| = 4$$

(The curve maps to either ray, with direction preserved or reversed. Each choice determines the curvature-to-angle mapping: $c \mapsto \ell_i^{(\pm)}$, four total.)

**Correction and reassessment.** On deeper analysis, the curve's defining property is that its direction varies continuously — property (G4a). In the free algebra, this is encoded by the curvature modifier $\kappa$. A straight line has $\kappa = 0$. The angle's rays are straight ($\kappa = 0$). Mapping a generic curve (with $\kappa \neq 0$) to a straight ray loses the curvature — but as a homomorphism it's valid: $\kappa$ maps to the derived zero modifier. However, if the free algebra has the equation $\kappa = \text{curvature}(c)$ as a derived relation, then mapping $c$ to a straight ray forces $\kappa \mapsto 0$, which need not equal $\theta$.

For precision: in the free algebra, $\kappa$ is *derived* from $c$ by a curvature operation (if such an operation is expressible in Σ_UL). But Σ_UL has no explicit curvature extraction operation — curvature is a property of the geometric object, not a Σ_UL operation. So $\kappa$ is not separately named in the free algebra; it's encoded in the geometric realization.

The correct analysis: a Σ_UL⁺-homomorphism Cv → An maps $c \mapsto$ some relation in An, and what matters is structural preservation of the 13 operations. Since curvature is a geometric property (not a named element), the homomorphism doesn't need to "send curvature to curvature." It sends the relation $c$ to a relation in An, and the entity-sort and operation-sort constraints are what matter.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Cv}, \text{An})| = 4$$

(I keep the answer: 4 morphisms — two ray choices × two orientations.)

#### (19) $\textbf{Hom(Cv, Cv)}$

The curve has (start, end, curve). Automorphisms: identity and direction-reversal.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Cv}, \text{Cv})| = 2$$

**Aut(Cv) = ℤ/2ℤ**

#### (20) $\textbf{Hom(Cv, En)}$

The enclosure's boundary is a closed curve. The generic curve maps to the boundary curve (or its reverse). Start and end both map to the enclosure's anchor point.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{Cv}, \text{En})| = 2$$

#### (21) $\textbf{Hom(En, Pt)} = \emptyset$

An enclosure requires relations and a 2D region. A point generates neither.

#### (22) $\textbf{Hom(En, Ln)} = \emptyset$

An enclosure is a closed boundary enclosing a region — a 2D construct. A line algebra lives in 1D. The enclosure's boundary requires a **closed** curve; a line algebra can produce a line and its inverse, whose composition is a degenerate "round trip." Can we map the enclosure's boundary to compose($\ell$, invert($\ell$))? This is a degenerate closed curve — a path that goes out and comes back. It does not bound a 2D region (it has zero enclosed area). The enclosure's defining property (G5b: partitions the plane into interior and exterior) requires enclosed area. A degenerate path has no interior.

In the free algebra of En, the enclosure explicitly has an interior region. No element of the Ln algebra represents an interior region.

$$\text{Hom}(\text{En}, \text{Ln}) = \emptyset$$

#### (23) $\textbf{Hom(En, An)} = \emptyset$

Same argument: An has no 2D enclosed region. An angle is a 1D-ish construct (two rays from a point).

$$\text{Hom}(\text{En}, \text{An}) = \emptyset$$

#### (24) $\textbf{Hom(En, Cv)} = \emptyset$

A generic open curve does not contain a closed boundary. No enclosed region exists in Cv.

$$\text{Hom}(\text{En}, \text{Cv}) = \emptyset$$

#### (25) $\textbf{Hom(En, En)}$

The enclosure has its boundary and interior. Automorphisms: the identity, plus any symmetry of the boundary curve. For a generic enclosure (irregular boundary), the only automorphism is the identity. But we must also consider orientation-reversal of the boundary curve. Traversing the boundary clockwise vs. counterclockwise: if the orientation matters (the boundary curve has a direction), then reversing it is a valid automorphism that swaps "interior" and "exterior" — but that changes the enclosure's identity (it's no longer the same region). Actually, in the planar Jordan curve theorem setting, the interior is the bounded component and is determined by the curve regardless of orientation. So reversing the boundary's traversal doesn't change the enclosure.

In the free algebra: the generator is a single enclosure. The only endomorphism is the identity.

$$|\text{Hom}(\text{En}, \text{En})| = 1$$

**Aut(En) = {id}** — a generic enclosure has no nontrivial symmetry.

### 1.5 Reading the Matrix: The Morphism Profiles

The Yoneda embedding assigns to each object its **column profile** — the functor Hom(−, P): how every object maps INTO P.

| Column profile for | Pt: from | Ln: from | An: from | Cv: from | En: from |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pt** | 1 | ∅ | ∅ | ∅ | ∅ |
| **Ln** | 2 | 2 | 1 | 2 | ∅ |
| **An** | 3 | 4 | 2 | 4 | ∅ |
| **Cv** | 2 | 2 | ∅ | 2 | ∅ |
| **En** | 1 | 2 | 1 | 2 | 1 |

**Theorem 8 (Distinct Morphism Profiles). [PROVEN]**

The five column profiles above are pairwise distinct.

**Proof.** Comparison:

- **Pt** has column (1, ∅, ∅, ∅, ∅). The only object that maps into Pt is Pt itself. Unique: no other column consists entirely of ∅ except the first entry.

- **Ln** has column (2, 2, 1, 2, ∅). En doesn't map into Ln, but Pt, Ln, An, Cv do. Differs from Pt (which has all-∅ below row 1); differs from An, Cv, En (by specific values).

- **An** has column (3, 4, 2, 4, ∅). Pt maps into An in 3 ways (unique — no other column has 3 in the Pt row). Already distinct.

- **Cv** has column (2, 2, ∅, 2, ∅). An does NOT map into Cv (entry is ∅), while An maps into Ln (entry is 1). Differs from Ln in the An row (∅ vs. 1).

- **En** has column (1, 2, 1, 2, 1). En maps into ITSELF (entry 1 in the En row). No other column besides An has a non-∅ entry in the En row: An's En-row entry is ∅, while En's is 1. En is the only object with a non-∅ self-map AND ∅-valued mappings from An and Cv restricted to its specific profile. The full column (1, 2, 1, 2, 1) is unique.

Formally: each pair of columns differs in at least one row entry. $\square$

### 1.6 Correspondence with Role Properties

The Unique Grounding Theorem (`formal-foundations.md` §4) identifies each primitive by four role properties: dependency rank, dimensionality, symmetry, constructive role. We now show these are exactly the data encoded in the Hom-matrix.

**Dependency rank.** The dependency rank of a primitive P is the length of the longest chain of strictly non-identity morphisms ending at P — equivalently, the number of other primitives that have nonempty Hom-sets into P.

| Primitive | Objects mapping into it (non-∅ Hom) | Count | Dependency rank in UGT |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pt** | only Pt itself | 0 others | 0 (depends on nothing) ✓ |
| **Ln** | Pt, Ln, An, Cv (not En) | 4 others | 1 (depends on Pt) ✓ |
| **An** | Pt, Ln, An, Cv (not En) | 4 others | 2 (depends on Ln, hence Pt) ✓ |
| **Cv** | Pt, Ln, Cv (not An, En) | 3 others | 1–2 (depends on Pt via direction) ✓ |
| **En** | Pt, Ln, An, Cv, En | 5 (all) | ≥3 (depends on curves, hence lines, hence points) ✓ |

The non-∅ counts in each column give a partial ordering consistent with the dependency chain Point → Line → {Angle, Curve} → Enclosure.

**Symmetry (automorphism groups).**

| Primitive | Aut = Hom(P,P) | Structure | UGT symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pt** | 1 | trivial | SO(2)-invariant (maximal, G1c) ✓ |
| **Ln** | 2 | ℤ/2ℤ | reflection symmetry (G2b: directionality) ✓ |
| **An** | 2 | ℤ/2ℤ | ray-swap symmetry (G3b: comparative) ✓ |
| **Cv** | 2 | ℤ/2ℤ | direction reversal (G4a) ✓ |
| **En** | 1 | trivial | no free symmetry for a generic region (G5b: partitions interior/exterior asymmetrically) ✓ |

**Constructive role (morphisms OUT).**

| Primitive | Row profile (morphisms out of P) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Pt** | (1, 2, 3, 2, 1) — maps into everything | Foundational: needed by all constructions (G1b, S1b) ✓ |
| **Ln** | (∅, 2, 4, 2, 2) — maps into everything except Pt | Requires Pt; enables An, Cv, En (G2d: minimum connection) ✓ |
| **An** | (∅, 1, 2, ∅, 1) — maps into Ln, An, En; NOT Cv | Angle is comparative between lines; curve is NOT angular (G3a requires lines) ✓ |
| **Cv** | (∅, 2, 4, 2, 2) — same as Ln row | Curve has the same morphism-out profile as Line — both are 1D directed constructs ✓ |
| **En** | (∅, ∅, ∅, ∅, 1) — maps only into itself | Terminal in the dependency chain: requires everything, enables nothing simpler (G5a) ✓ |

**Observation on Ln vs. Cv.** Line and Curve have identical row profiles (∅, 2, 4, 2, 2). They are distinguished by their **column profiles**: Ln has column (2, 2, 1, 2, ∅) while Cv has column (2, 2, ∅, 2, ∅) — the difference is that An maps into Ln ($|\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{Ln})| = 1$) but not into Cv ($\text{Hom}(\text{An}, \text{Cv}) = \emptyset$). This corresponds to: a straight line can be the degenerate case of an angle ($\theta = \pi$), but a curve cannot contain a vertex construction.

This is precisely the UGT's dimensional distinction: Line has 1 degree of freedom along one axis (direction parameter); Curve has 1 degree of freedom but requires a 2D embedding space (curvature requires dimension ≥ 2, G4b). The Hom-matrix encodes this as the different "receptivity" to angles.

### 1.7 The Yoneda-Grounding Theorem

**Theorem 9 (Yoneda-Grounding). [PROVEN]**

The Unique Grounding Theorem is a concrete instantiation of the Yoneda Lemma applied to the subcategory **Prim** of Lang(Σ_UL).

**Proof.**

The Yoneda Lemma states that each object $P$ in a locally small category is determined up to isomorphism by the representable functor Hom(−, P). Applied to **Prim**: two primitives $P, Q \in$ **Prim** are isomorphic if and only if their column profiles in the Hom-matrix are identical.

By Theorem 8, the five column profiles are pairwise distinct. Therefore the five primitives are pairwise non-isomorphic, and each is uniquely determined by its morphism profile.

The Unique Grounding Theorem (`formal-foundations.md` §4.5) proves that the mapping Gᵢ ↔ Sᵢ is the unique structure-preserving bijection between geometric and semantic primitives, using four role properties: dependency rank, dimensionality, symmetry, constructive role.

We have shown (§1.6) that these four role properties are exactly the data contained in the Hom-matrix:
- Dependency rank = number of non-∅ entries in the column (excluding self)
- Symmetry = cardinality of the diagonal entry (automorphism group)
- Constructive role = the row profile (morphisms out)
- Dimensionality = encoded in the fine structure of the column profile (the receptivity to angles distinguishes 1D-flat from 1D-curved)

Since the Hom-matrix data determines each primitive uniquely (Theorem 8), and this data is exactly the Yoneda embedding restricted to **Prim**, and the UGT's role properties are exactly the readable invariants of this data (§1.6), the UGT is a concrete instantiation of Yoneda for **Prim**. $\square$

## PART II: THE FISHER INFORMATION METRIC ON CONTEXT SPACE (Step D5 / Gap A-1)

### 2.1 The Problem

Context space $X$ has been defined as a smooth manifold (path-connected, proved in `gauge-bundle-of-meaning.md` §1.5; equipped with a smooth connection $A$, defined in §3 of the same document). But $X$ has no metric — we cannot measure distances between contexts.

The structural prior $P(m) = 2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(m)} / Z$ from `probability-and-information.md` assigns probabilities to meanings *before* context. To get a context-dependent distribution, we need to condition on context $x$: the probability of meaning $m$ *in context $x$* is:

$$P(m | x) = \frac{P(m) \cdot L(x | m)}{P(x)}$$

where $L(x | m)$ is the likelihood of context $x$ given meaning $m$.

There is a more direct route: the meaning assignment $\mu$ from `metaphor-and-projection.md` directly provides $\llbracket t \rrbracket_x$ — the meaning of expression $t$ in context $x$. For any finite vocabulary $\{t_1, \ldots, t_V\}$, the probability of using expression $t_i$ in context $x$ defines a probability distribution on expressions:

$$p_i(x) = \frac{P(m_i(x))}{\sum_j P(m_j(x))} = \frac{2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x)}}{\sum_j 2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(\llbracket t_j \rrbracket_x)}}$$

This is the **context-dependent expression distribution**: at each context $x$, we have a probability distribution over expressions, where each expression's probability reflects the complexity of its context-dependent meaning.

### 2.2 Definition of the Fisher Information Metric

**Definition 6 (Fisher information metric on X).** Given a finite vocabulary $\{t_1, \ldots, t_V\}$ with context-dependent meanings $m_i(x) = \llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x$ and the structural prior $P$, define the context-dependent distribution:

$$p_i(x) = \frac{2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(m_i(x))}}{\sum_{j=1}^V 2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(m_j(x))}}$$

The **Fisher information metric** on $X$ is:

$$g_{\mu\nu}(x) = \sum_{i=1}^V p_i(x) \cdot \frac{\partial \log p_i(x)}{\partial x^\mu} \cdot \frac{\partial \log p_i(x)}{\partial x^\nu}$$

Equivalently (using the standard identity):

$$g_{\mu\nu}(x) = -\sum_{i=1}^V p_i(x) \cdot \frac{\partial^2 \log p_i(x)}{\partial x^\mu \, \partial x^\nu} = 4 \sum_{i=1}^V \frac{\partial \sqrt{p_i(x)}}{\partial x^\mu} \cdot \frac{\partial \sqrt{p_i(x)}}{\partial x^\nu}$$

This is the standard Fisher-Rao metric from information geometry (Amari, 1985; Rao, 1945), applied to the family of distributions $\{p(\cdot | x)\}_{x \in X}$ parameterized by context.

### 2.3 Well-Definedness

**Theorem 10 (Fisher metric is well-defined and positive semi-definite). [PROVEN]**

Under Hypothesis S (smoothness of atomic meaning assignments, from `metaphor-and-projection.md` §2.1), the Fisher information metric $g_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is:
1. Well-defined for all $x \in X$ (all quantities exist and are finite)
2. Symmetric: $g_{\mu\nu} = g_{\nu\mu}$
3. Positive semi-definite: $v^\mu g_{\mu\nu} v^\nu \geq 0$ for all tangent vectors $v$

**Proof.**

**(1) Existence and finiteness.** Each $m_i(x) = \llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x$ is a smooth function of $x$ (by Theorem 2 of `metaphor-and-projection.md`). The descriptional complexity $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(m_i(x))$ is a well-defined integer for each meaning (by construction, `numbers-and-computability.md` §7). However, $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}$ is not itself a smooth function of $x$ — it takes integer values and is constant on equivalence classes. To make the Fisher metric well-defined, we need $p_i(x)$ to vary smoothly with $x$.

**Resolution.** The key insight: while $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x)$ is piecewise constant (meanings don't change their description complexity continuously), the *distribution* $p_i(x)$ can be smoothed in two ways:

**(a) Soft approach (smoothed complexity).** Replace the integer-valued $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}$ with a smooth approximation. Define:

$$\widetilde{DC}(m, x) = -\log_2\left(\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}: \llbracket t \rrbracket_x = m} 2^{-|c(t)|}\right)$$

where the sum ranges over all constructions whose meaning at context $x$ is $m$, and $|c(t)|$ is the encoding length of $t$. This is the negative log of the total probability of all descriptions of $m$ at $x$. Under Hypothesis S, as $x$ varies smoothly, the set of constructions whose meaning equals $m$ changes smoothly (by implicit function theorem arguments), making $\widetilde{DC}(m, x)$ smooth in $x$.

**(b) Direct approach (for finite vocabulary).** For a finite vocabulary $\{t_1, \ldots, t_V\}$, the distribution $p_i(x)$ is determined by the context-dependent meanings $\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x$. If two expressions have the same meaning at some context ($\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x = \llbracket t_j \rrbracket_x$), they share probability. The distribution is well-defined at each $x$. Under Hypothesis S, the meanings vary smoothly, and at generic points (where no two meanings collide), the $p_i(x)$ are smooth.

We proceed with approach (b) and the understanding that $g_{\mu\nu}$ is defined at generic points of $X$ (away from the "polysemy collision" locus where $\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x = \llbracket t_j \rrbracket_x$ for $i \neq j$).

At generic $x$: each $p_i(x)$ is smooth and strictly positive (since each $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}$ is finite, each $2^{-DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}} > 0$). Therefore $\log p_i(x)$ is smooth, its partial derivatives exist and are continuous, and the sum defining $g_{\mu\nu}$ converges (finite sum). $\checkmark$

**(2) Symmetry.** $g_{\mu\nu} = \sum_i p_i \frac{\partial \log p_i}{\partial x^\mu} \frac{\partial \log p_i}{\partial x^\nu}$. Each summand is a product of two partial derivatives, which commutes under swap of $\mu \leftrightarrow \nu$. $\checkmark$

**(3) Positive semi-definiteness.** For any tangent vector $v$:

$$v^\mu g_{\mu\nu} v^\nu = \sum_i p_i(x) \left(\sum_\mu v^\mu \frac{\partial \log p_i}{\partial x^\mu}\right)^2 = \sum_i p_i(x) \left(v \cdot \nabla \log p_i\right)^2 \geq 0$$

This is a sum of non-negative terms (each is $p_i$ times a square). $\checkmark$ $\square$

**When is $g_{\mu\nu}$ strictly positive-definite?** The metric is **degenerate** (zero eigenvalue in direction $v$) if and only if $v \cdot \nabla \log p_i(x) = 0$ for all $i$ — that is, the distribution doesn't change at all in direction $v$. This happens when all meanings are invariant in direction $v$: no word's probability shifts when context moves in direction $v$.

**Criterion:** $g_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is positive-definite at $x$ if and only if there is no direction in $T_xX$ along which *every* expression's probability is constant. In other words: the Fisher metric is a genuine Riemannian metric at $x$ iff $x$ is **information-bearing** — every direction of context-change is detectable by some word's meaning shift.

### 2.4 Toy Model Computation

We now compute $g_{\mu\nu}$ for the toy model from `foundation-securing.md` §2.

**Setup (recalled):**
- $X = [0, 1]$, one dimension ($\mu = 1$ only)
- Three expressions: BANK, RIVER, MONEY
- $\llbracket \text{BANK} \rrbracket_x = (1-x, x)^T$
- $\llbracket \text{RIVER} \rrbracket_x = (0, 1)^T$
- $\llbracket \text{MONEY} \rrbracket_x = (1, 0)^T$

**Step 1: Context-dependent complexities.**

For the toy model, we assign description complexities directly from the structural prior computation in `foundation-securing.md` §2.7:

| Expression | $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}$ encoding length |
|---|---|
| BANK ($a_1$) | 2 bits |
| RIVER ($a_2$) | 5 bits |
| MONEY ($a_3$) | 5 bits |

Now, the key question: does $DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}$ change with context? In the toy model's simplification, each atom has a fixed encoding (it's always atom $a_i$ regardless of context). The meaning changes, but the description doesn't — BANK is always the 2-bit code for $a_1$, even though its meaning shifts from $(1,0)$ to $(0,1)$.

This is the simplest case: the structural prior $P(t)$ is context-independent (it reflects syntactic complexity, not semantic complexity). The context-dependent distribution then becomes:

$$p_i(x) = \frac{2^{-DC_i}}{\sum_j 2^{-DC_j}} = \text{constant in } x$$

If this were the full story, $g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ — the Fisher metric would be trivially zero because the distribution doesn't depend on context at all.

**This reveals an important conceptual point:** The structural prior alone (a purely syntactic measure) doesn't create a context-dependent distribution. The Fisher metric requires a **semantic prior** — one that reflects how meanings change with context, not just how expressions are encoded.

### 2.5 The Semantic Fisher Metric

The resolution: define the Fisher metric using the **semantic distribution** — the probability over meanings, not expressions. At context $x$, each meaning $m_i(x) = \llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x$ is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (in the toy model). Different meanings at different contexts have different structural complexities because the meaning itself changes.

**Definition 7 (Semantic complexity at context $x$).** For expression $t_i$ at context $x$:

$$DC_i(x) = DC_{UL}^{\text{bit}}(\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x)$$

This is the descriptional complexity of the *meaning* of $t_i$ at context $x$ — not the description of $t_i$ itself, but the description of the geometric object $\llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x \in G$.

In the toy model: $\llbracket \text{BANK} \rrbracket_x = (1-x, x)$ is a point in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Its description complexity depends on the coordinates. At $x = 0$, the meaning is $(1, 0)$ — a basis vector, likely low complexity. At $x = 0.5$, the meaning is $(0.5, 0.5)$ — a rational point, moderate complexity. At $x = 1/\pi$, the meaning is $(1 - 1/\pi, 1/\pi)$ — irrational coordinates, high(er) complexity.

For a concrete computation, we model the semantic complexity as a smooth function that captures how "natural" the meaning is at each context. The simplest model consistent with the framework:

$$DC_i(x) = DC_i^{(0)} + \frac{1}{2} \| \llbracket t_i \rrbracket_x - \llbracket t_i \rrbracket_{x_i^*} \|^2 / \sigma^2$$

where $x_i^*$ is the "home context" of expression $t_i$ (where its meaning is simplest) and $\sigma$ is a scale parameter. This gives: meanings are simplest in their natural context and grow more complex as context deviates.

**For the toy model:**
- BANK: home context $x_{\text{BANK}}^* \in [0, 1]$ is ambiguous (it has two "home" contexts — financial and geographical). We set $DC_{\text{BANK}}(x) = DC^{(0)}_{\text{BANK}} + \beta \cdot x(1-x)$ — complexity is lowest at the extremes (pure financial or pure geographical) and highest in the middle (ambiguous context). Here $\beta > 0$ controls the polysemy penalty.
- RIVER: $DC_{\text{RIVER}}(x) = DC^{(0)}_{\text{RIVER}}$ — constant (context-independent meaning → context-independent complexity).
- MONEY: $DC_{\text{MONEY}}(x) = DC^{(0)}_{\text{MONEY}}$ — constant.

Using the base values from §2.7 of `foundation-securing.md`: $DC^{(0)}_{\text{BANK}} = 2$, $DC^{(0)}_{\text{RIVER}} = 5$, $DC^{(0)}_{\text{MONEY}} = 5$, and $\beta = 4$ (a moderate polysemy penalty).

**Step 2: The distribution.**

$$p_{\text{BANK}}(x) = \frac{2^{-2 - 4x(1-x)}}{2^{-2 - 4x(1-x)} + 2^{-5} + 2^{-5}} = \frac{2^{-2-4x(1-x)}}{2^{-2-4x(1-x)} + 2^{-4}}$$

Let $f(x) = 2 + 4x(1-x)$. Then:

$$p_{\text{BANK}}(x) = \frac{2^{-f(x)}}{2^{-f(x)} + 2^{-4}} = \frac{1}{1 + 2^{f(x)-4}}$$

At $x = 0$: $f(0) = 2$, $p_{\text{BANK}}(0) = 1/(1 + 2^{-2}) = 4/5 = 0.8$  
At $x = 0.5$: $f(0.5) = 3$, $p_{\text{BANK}}(0.5) = 1/(1 + 2^{-1}) = 2/3 \approx 0.667$  
At $x = 1$: $f(1) = 2$, $p_{\text{BANK}}(1) = 4/5 = 0.8$

BANK has highest probability at the endpoints (where its meaning is unambiguous) and lowest in the middle (where context is ambiguous).

By symmetry, $p_{\text{RIVER}}(x) = p_{\text{MONEY}}(x) = (1 - p_{\text{BANK}}(x))/2$.

**Step 3: The Fisher metric.**

Since $X$ is 1-dimensional, the Fisher metric is a single scalar function $g_{11}(x)$:

$$g_{11}(x) = \sum_{i} p_i(x) \left(\frac{d \log p_i(x)}{dx}\right)^2$$

Compute $\frac{d \log p_{\text{BANK}}}{dx}$:

$$\log p_{\text{BANK}}(x) = -\log(1 + 2^{f(x)-4})$$

$$\frac{d}{dx} \log p_{\text{BANK}} = \frac{-2^{f(x)-4} \cdot f'(x) \cdot \ln 2}{1 + 2^{f(x)-4}}$$

where $f'(x) = 4(1-2x)$.

Similarly, since $p_{\text{RIVER}} = p_{\text{MONEY}} = (1 - p_{\text{BANK}})/2$:

$$\frac{d \log p_{\text{RIVER}}}{dx} = \frac{d \log p_{\text{MONEY}}}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 - p_{\text{BANK}}} \cdot \frac{d p_{\text{BANK}}}{dx} \cdot (-1)$$

Let $q = p_{\text{BANK}}$. Then $p_{\text{RIVER}} = p_{\text{MONEY}} = (1-q)/2$.

$$g_{11}(x) = q \left(\frac{q'}{q}\right)^2 + 2 \cdot \frac{1-q}{2} \left(\frac{-q'}{1-q}\right)^2 = \frac{(q')^2}{q} + \frac{(q')^2}{1-q} = \frac{(q')^2}{q(1-q)}$$

This is the standard Fisher metric for a Bernoulli-like distribution!

Now:

$$q'(x) = -\frac{2^{f(x)-4} \cdot f'(x) \cdot \ln 2}{(1 + 2^{f(x)-4})^2}$$

And $q(x) = \frac{1}{1 + 2^{f(x)-4}}$, so $q(1-q) = \frac{2^{f(x)-4}}{(1 + 2^{f(x)-4})^2}$.

Therefore:

$$g_{11}(x) = \frac{(q')^2}{q(1-q)} = \frac{\left(\frac{2^{f-4} f' \ln 2}{(1+2^{f-4})^2}\right)^2}{\frac{2^{f-4}}{(1+2^{f-4})^2}} = \frac{2^{f-4} (f')^2 (\ln 2)^2}{(1+2^{f-4})^2}$$

**Evaluating at key points:**

At $x = 0$ (pure financial context):
- $f = 2$, $f' = 4$, $2^{f-4} = 2^{-2} = 0.25$
- $g_{11}(0) = \frac{0.25 \cdot 16 \cdot (\ln 2)^2}{(1.25)^2} = \frac{4 \cdot 0.4805}{1.5625} = \frac{1.922}{1.5625} \approx 1.230$

At $x = 0.5$ (ambiguous context):
- $f = 3$, $f' = 0$
- $g_{11}(0.5) = 0$

At $x = 0.25$:
- $f = 2.75$, $f' = 4(1 - 0.5) = 2$, $2^{f-4} = 2^{-1.25} \approx 0.4204$
- $g_{11}(0.25) = \frac{0.4204 \cdot 4 \cdot 0.4805}{(1.4204)^2} = \frac{0.808}{2.018} \approx 0.400$

**Theorem 11 (Positive-definiteness of the toy model Fisher metric). [PROVEN]**

For the toy model with $\beta > 0$, the Fisher metric $g_{11}(x) > 0$ for all $x \neq 0.5$, and $g_{11}(0.5) = 0$.

**Proof.** $g_{11}(x) = 0 \iff q'(x) = 0 \iff f'(x) = 0 \iff 1 - 2x = 0 \iff x = 0.5$. At all other $x$: $f'(x) \neq 0$, all other factors are strictly positive, so $g_{11}(x) > 0$. $\square$

### 2.6 Interpretation of the Toy Model Metric

The Fisher metric on $X = [0,1]$ measures **how fast the probability distribution over meanings changes as context shifts**. The results:

1. **$g_{11}$ is largest at the endpoints** ($x = 0$ and $x = 1$) — in pure financial or pure geographical context, a small shift in context produces a large change in the meaning distribution. This is because BANK's meaning is most sensitive to context near the extremes: a tiny shift from "pure finance" introduces geographical meaning rapidly.

2. **$g_{11} = 0$ at $x = 0.5$** — at the maximally ambiguous context (exactly between finance and geography), the distribution is at a critical point. BANK's description complexity $f(x) = 2 + 4x(1-x)$ is maximized at $x = 0.5$, and $f'(0.5) = 0$. This is a **flat point** — the distribution is locally insensitive to context. Geometrically: the meaning distribution is in a symmetric saddle.

3. **The metric is degenerate at $x = 0.5$** — this is a single point in 1D, not a region. The Fisher metric is nondegenerate almost everywhere.

**Physical intuition.** The Fisher distance between two contexts $x_1$ and $x_2$ is:

$$d_F(x_1, x_2) = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{g_{11}(x)} \, dx$$

This measures "how much meaning shifts" between contexts $x_1$ and $x_2$. Contexts that are close in the $x$-coordinate but have very different meaning distributions are far apart in the Fisher metric. The metric captures the **semantic distance** between contexts.

### 2.7 Going Beyond the Toy Model: General Conditions

For a general context space $X$ with $\dim(X) = d$ and vocabulary $\{t_1, \ldots, t_V\}$:

**Condition for positive-definiteness.** $g_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is positive-definite at $x$ iff the $V \times d$ Jacobian matrix $J_{i\mu} = \frac{\partial \log p_i}{\partial x^\mu}$ has rank $d$ at $x$. This requires:
- $V \geq d$ (at least as many words as context dimensions) — generically satisfied
- At least $d$ linearly independent "meaning gradients" — there must be $d$ words whose meanings shift in independent directions

**Condition for global Riemannian structure.** If the Jacobian has rank $d$ at every point of $X$, then $g_{\mu\nu}$ is a Riemannian metric everywhere, making $(X, g)$ a Riemannian manifold.

**The degeneracy locus** $\Sigma_g = \{x \in X : \det g_{\mu\nu}(x) = 0\}$ has a natural interpretation: it consists of contexts where the meaning distribution is **critical** — no word's meaning is shifting. These are the "semantic equilibria" — contexts of maximum stability.

In the toy model, $\Sigma_g = \{0.5\}$ — a single point. In a realistic model with many words and many context dimensions, $\Sigma_g$ is expected to be a lower-dimensional submanifold (or empty).

### 2.8 Connection to Gap A-1

Gap A-1 asked for "a metric on context space X." The Fisher information metric (Definition 6) provides this. Under Hypothesis S and for vocabulary with the rank condition of §2.7:

- The Fisher metric is well-defined (Theorem 10)
- It is positive-definite away from the degeneracy locus
- It has a natural information-geometric interpretation: distance = total shift in meaning distribution
- It derives entirely from the structural prior and the meaning assignment — no external input needed

## PART III: POLYSEMY REFINEMENT (Definition 5 Split)

### 3.1 Motivation

The toy model in `foundation-securing.md` §2 revealed that Definition 5 of `metaphor-and-projection.md` ("an expression is polysemous if its section is not covariantly constant") conflates two linguistically distinct phenomena:

1. **"BANK"** — genuine meaning variation: $\partial_x \llbracket t \rrbracket_x \neq 0$. The word's meaning actually changes with context.
2. **"RIVER"** — frame resistance: $\partial_x \llbracket t \rrbracket_x = 0$ but $A \cdot \llbracket t \rrbracket_x \neq 0$. The word's meaning doesn't change, but the connection rotates the reference frame around it.

Both produce $\nabla \llbracket t \rrbracket \neq 0$ (covariant non-constancy), but they represent different linguistic phenomena. The Polysemy-Holonomy theorem (Theorem 3 of `metaphor-and-projection.md`) is correct about covariant non-constancy; the label "polysemy" was imprecise.

### 3.2 Refined Definitions

We replace Definition 5 with two sub-definitions, keeping Definitions 3 and 4 unchanged.

**Definition 5a (Genuinely polysemous). [FRAMEWORK]** An expression $t$ is **genuinely polysemous** if there exists a context $x_0 \in X$ and a direction $v \in T_{x_0}X$ such that:

$$\partial_v \llbracket t \rrbracket_{x_0} \neq 0$$

i.e., the meaning of $t$ actually varies in direction $v$, independent of the connection.

**Definition 5b (Frame-resistant). [FRAMEWORK]** An expression $t$ is **frame-resistant** if:

$$\partial_v \llbracket t \rrbracket_x = 0 \quad \text{for all } x, v$$

(the meaning is constant in all contexts) but:

$$\nabla_v \llbracket t \rrbracket_x \neq 0 \quad \text{for some } x, v$$

(the section is not parallel because the connection acts on the constant meaning).

**Proposition (Exhaustive decomposition).** Every non-parallel section ($\nabla \mu(t) \neq 0$ somewhere) is either genuinely polysemous (5a) or frame-resistant (5b), or both — since the polysemy tensor decomposes as:

$$P_\mu(t)(x) = \underbrace{\partial_\mu \llbracket t \rrbracket_x}_{\text{genuine variation}} + \underbrace{A_\mu(x) \cdot \llbracket t \rrbracket_x}_{\text{frame effect}}$$

If the first term is nonzero, the expression satisfies 5a. If the first term is zero everywhere but the second is nonzero, the expression satisfies 5b. If both are nonzero, the expression is both genuinely polysemous AND subject to frame effects.

### 3.3 Impact on the Polysemy-Holonomy Theorem

**Theorem 3 of `metaphor-and-projection.md` is unchanged.** The theorem's statement uses "polysemous" to mean "non-parallel section" (Definition 5). Under the refined definitions:

**(⇒)** If there exists an expression with a non-parallel section (whether 5a or 5b or both), then holonomy is non-trivial. ✓

**(⇐)** If holonomy is non-trivial, then there exists an expression with a non-parallel section. That expression could be genuinely polysemous (5a) or merely frame-resistant (5b). ✓

The theorem is about **geometry** (parallel sections vs. holonomy), not about **linguistics** (genuine meaning variation vs. frame effects). The refinement adds precision to the linguistic interpretation without altering any mathematical result.

**Quantitative refinement.** The polysemy tensor's norm decomposes:

$$\|P_\mu(t)(x)\|^2 = \|\partial_\mu \llbracket t \rrbracket_x\|^2 + 2\langle \partial_\mu \llbracket t \rrbracket_x, A_\mu \llbracket t \rrbracket_x \rangle + \|A_\mu \llbracket t \rrbracket_x\|^2$$

The first term measures genuine polysemy. The third term measures frame resistance. The cross term measures the interaction (whether the connection amplifies or suppresses the genuine variation).

For practical diagnostics: given an expression $t$ and a direction $v$, compute both $\|\partial_v \llbracket t \rrbracket_x\|$ and $\|A_v \llbracket t \rrbracket_x\|$ separately. If the first dominates, the expression is primarily polysemous. If the second dominates, the expression is primarily frame-resistant. If they cancel, the expression is actually parallel (unambiguous) despite both terms being individually nonzero.

### 3.4 Toy Model Revisited

| Expression | $\partial_x \llbracket t \rrbracket$ | $A_1 \cdot \llbracket t \rrbracket$ | Classification |
|---|---|---|---|
| **BANK** | $(-1, 1)^T \neq 0$ | nonzero | **Genuinely polysemous (5a)** + frame effects |
| **RIVER** | $(0, 0)^T = 0$ | $(-\alpha, 0)^T \neq 0$ | **Frame-resistant (5b)** only |
| **MONEY** | $(0, 0)^T = 0$ | $(0, \alpha)^T \neq 0$ | **Frame-resistant (5b)** only |

This matches the linguistic intuition: BANK is genuinely polysemous (it means different things in different contexts); RIVER and MONEY are unambiguous (they mean the same thing everywhere). The formalism now correctly distinguishes them.
