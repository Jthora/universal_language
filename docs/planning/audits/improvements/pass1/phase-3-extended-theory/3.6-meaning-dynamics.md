## The Gap

UL currently describes meaning as a STATIC structure. But meaning changes:
- **Semantic drift:** Words change meaning over time ("awful" = full of awe → terrible)
- **Conceptual blending:** Two concepts merge to create a new one (Fauconnier & Turner)
- **Learning:** A speaker's meaning-space restructures as they learn
- **Communication:** Two speakers' meaning-spaces interact and partially align during conversation
- **Cultural evolution:** Shared meanings evolve under selection pressures

These are DYNAMICAL processes. The current framework has no equations of motion, no notion of time evolution, no attractors or bifurcations.

Gap-analysis.md §1.7: "No theory of how meaning representations change, merge, or evolve."

## The Mathematical Framework: Flow Equations on Meaning-Space

If meaning-space has Riemannian geometry (Task 3.3), it supports differential equations. The dynamics of meaning could be modeled as:

$$\frac{d\phi}{dt} = F(\phi, \nabla\phi, g)$$

where $\phi$ is a meaning configuration, $\nabla$ is the covariant derivative, $g$ is the metric, and $F$ is a force law determined by:
- **Communication:** Alignment pressure between interlocutors
- **Pragmatic pressure:** Optimization for relevance (Task 3.5)
- **Convention:** Social pressure toward shared meanings
- **Innovation:** Random or directed exploration of meaning-space

## Research Questions

### Q1: What are the equations of motion for meaning?

**Candidate models:**

**Model A: Gradient flow.** Meaning evolves to minimize an "energy" functional:
$$\frac{d\phi}{dt} = -\nabla E(\phi)$$
where $E$ measures something like: mismatch between intended and interpreted meaning. This gives stable equilibria (shared meanings) and basins of attraction (dialects/register).

**Model B: Ricci flow analogy.** Meaning-space itself evolves:
$$\frac{dg}{dt} = -2\text{Ric}(g) + \lambda g$$
where $g$ is the metric on meaning-space and Ric is the Ricci curvature. This would model how the GEOMETRY of meaning changes (curvature smoothing = meaning simplification; singularity formation = semantic split).

**Model C: Population dynamics.** Multiple agents have meaning-states, and meanings evolve via replicator dynamics (evolutionary game theory). This captures language change as a population-level phenomenon.

**Model D: Stochastic diffusion.** Meaning undergoes Brownian motion on meaning-space:
$$d\phi = \mu(\phi) dt + \sigma(\phi) dW$$
where $\mu$ is drift (systematic meaning change) and $\sigma$ is diffusion (random variation). This is the most general and connects to Task 3.1 (probability).

### Q2: Are there attractors?

A meaning attractor is a configuration toward which nearby meanings evolve. Candidates:
- **Prototypes:** Category centers (Rosch's prototype theory)
- **Conventions:** Shared meanings stabilized by coordination pressure
- **Archetypes:** Deep structural patterns that persist across cultures

If meaning dynamics has attractors, this would explain why certain meanings recur across unrelated languages (Wierzbicka's semantic primes) — they're attractors of the meaning flow.

### Q3: Are there bifurcations?

A bifurcation is a qualitative change in the dynamics when a parameter crosses a threshold. In meaning dynamics:
- **Polysemy formation:** A word's meaning splits into two distinct senses (bifurcation from one attractor to two)
- **Metaphor conventionalization:** A novel metaphor becomes literal (an unstable meaning path becomes a stable attractor)
- **Language death:** A meaning system loses all speakers (attractor destruction)

Bifurcation analysis would predict WHEN and HOW meaning changes occur — not just that they do.

### Q4: Can communication be modeled as coupled dynamics?

Two speakers in conversation have coupled meaning-states $(\phi_A, \phi_B)$:

$$\frac{d\phi_A}{dt} = F(\phi_A) + \kappa(\phi_B - \phi_A)$$
$$\frac{d\phi_B}{dt} = F(\phi_B) + \kappa(\phi_A - \phi_B)$$

where $\kappa$ is the coupling strength (how much speakers accommodate each other). This predicts:
- **Convergence:** Speakers align over time ($\kappa$ large)
- **Disagreement:** Speakers diverge ($\kappa$ small; repulsive dynamics)
- **Misunderstanding:** Non-trivial fixed points of the coupled system

### Q5: Does semantic drift have a geometric signature?

Historical linguistics studies sound change and meaning change over centuries. If meaning dynamics is governed by flow equations, historical semantic drift should follow geodesics (or near-geodesics) in meaning-space.

**Test:** Take historical meaning shifts (e.g., "nice": foolish → pleasant → precise) and check whether the trajectory follows a geodesic in the Riemannian meaning-space from Task 3.3.

## Approach

### Phase A: Define the dynamics

1. Choose a candidate model (A, B, C, or D) based on mathematical tractability
2. Define the force law / evolution equation explicitly in terms of Σ_UL operations
3. Prove: solutions exist and are well-defined (basic existence/uniqueness for the ODE/PDE)

### Phase B: Attractor analysis

1. Find fixed points of the dynamics
2. Classify stability (stable, unstable, saddle)
3. Interpret: which fixed points correspond to recognizable meaning structures?

### Phase C: Bifurcation analysis

1. Identify parameters that control qualitative behavior
2. Find bifurcation points (where attractor structure changes)
3. Model at least one linguistic bifurcation: polysemy formation or metaphor conventionalization

### Phase D: Coupled dynamics and communication

1. Formulate the coupled system for two agents
2. Analyze convergence conditions
3. Model at least one conversational scenario: agreement, misunderstanding, persuasion

### Phase E: Historical validation

1. Take 3-5 documented historical semantic drifts
2. Plot proposed trajectories in meaning-space
3. Assess: are they geodesic? Are they consistent with the dynamics?

## Deliverable

1. Equations of motion for meaning (one well-justified model)
2. Attractor classification for the chosen model
3. At least one bifurcation computed
4. Coupled dynamics for two communicating agents
5. Comparison with at least one historical semantic drift

- The historical validation (Phase E) may require computational resources and linguistic datasets beyond the project's scope
