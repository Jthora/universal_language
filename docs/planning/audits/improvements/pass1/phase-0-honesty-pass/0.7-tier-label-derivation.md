## Convention Classification

### FORCED
The semantic interpretation is the only possible one given the algebraic structure. These are rare.

**Example:** `predicate : e × r × e → a` — the operation that combines an entity, a relation, and an entity into an assertion. This is forced because it's the only operation with signature e × r × e → a. There's nothing else it COULD mean.

### CONSTRAINED
Multiple interpretations exist, but structural properties (involution, commutativity, etc.) narrow the field to a small number. The chosen interpretation is the most natural.

**Example:** `negate : a → a` implemented as reflection. Reflection is an involution (applying it twice returns to the original). Negation is an involution (¬¬p = p). This constrains negate to involutory transformations. But path reversal is also involutory. The identification of negate with reflection (rather than some other involution) is constrained but not forced.

### CONVENTIONAL
The semantic interpretation is one of several plausible choices. The chosen one is motivated by analogy or intuition, not by algebraic constraint.

**Example:** `quantify : m × e → a` implemented as scaling. "Big" ≈ "all" and "small" ≈ "some" is a cognitive metaphor (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980), not a geometric necessity. Area-based quantification (larger area = larger quantity) is intuitive but not structurally forced. Alternative: quantification as counting (number of copies) or as density (points per unit area).
