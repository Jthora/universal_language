# Target Effect Score (TES) Rubric — 0 to 5

**Purpose:** Scores whether the *specific* cognitive effect targeted by each artifact actually appeared in the output.  
**Usage:** Score TES only on the 4 targeted tasks (Tpat, Thier, Tabs, Tform). TES is NOT scored on general tasks (T1, T2, T5).  
**Relationship to M1–M5:** TES is scored AFTER M1–M5, as an additional metric. It is possible to have high M1–M5 with low TES (good cross-domain reasoning that doesn't match the predicted target effect) or low M1–M5 with high TES (weak overall quality but the specific effect appeared).

---

## TES for Tpat (Pattern Recognition Task)

**Target effect:** Identification of formal structural isomorphisms across disconnected domains.  
**What to look for:** Does the output identify genuine structural correspondences (not surface analogies)? Does it articulate WHY the parallels exist at a formal level?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | No structural parallels identified; output discusses the two domains separately |
| 1 | Surface analogies only ("plate tectonics is *like* economics because both involve movement") |
| 2 | One genuine structural parallel identified but not formalized; explanation is intuitive rather than structural |
| 3 | Two or more structural parallels with some formal justification; at least one parallel identifies corresponding elements and operations |
| 4 | Three structural parallels with clear formal correspondence; explains what is preserved under the mapping |
| 5 | Three or more parallels articulated as explicit structure-preserving maps; identifies conditions under which the mapping breaks down; produces a genuine insight from the formal correspondence |

## TES for Thier (Hierarchical Decomposition Task)

**Target effect:** Recursive multi-level decomposition with compositional reconstruction.  
**What to look for:** Does the output actually decompose at 5 distinct levels? Are the levels genuinely hierarchical (not just different aspects)? Does it show how components compose to produce the level above?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | No decomposition; output provides a flat discussion of democracy |
| 1 | Some decomposition attempted but levels are not clearly distinguished; fewer than 3 levels |
| 2 | 3–4 levels identified but composition is unclear; levels may overlap or skip too far |
| 3 | 5 levels present and genuinely hierarchical; composition shown for at least 2 transitions between levels |
| 4 | 5 clearly distinct levels from concrete to abstract; composition is explicit at each transition; the primitive level is genuinely fundamental |
| 5 | 5 levels with rigorous compositional reconstruction; the reader could *rebuild* the concept from the bottom-level primitives; the decomposition reveals something non-obvious about democracy |

## TES for Tabs (Abstraction Navigation Task)

**Target effect:** Restating a concrete fact at progressively higher levels of abstraction while preserving core truth.  
**What to look for:** Are the 5 levels genuinely at different abstraction levels (not just rephrased)? Is the truth preserved at each level? Does the highest level capture something genuinely abstract?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | No abstraction navigation; output discusses the statement without restating it at different levels |
| 1 | Output attempts level-shifting but produces paraphrases, not genuine abstractions; all levels are roughly at the same abstraction |
| 2 | 3–4 genuine levels of abstraction but the truth is lost at the higher levels or the highest level isn't truly abstract |
| 3 | 5 levels present; at least 3 are genuinely at different abstraction levels; truth is roughly preserved |
| 4 | 5 distinct levels from physical chemistry to abstract principle; truth is clearly preserved; each level is identifiably more abstract than the previous |
| 5 | 5 levels with explicit articulation of what is preserved and what is lost at each level-shift; the highest level articulates a genuinely universal principle; the path from concrete to abstract is illuminating |

## TES for Tform (Meaning Formation Task)

**Target effect:** Description of meaning formation as a dynamic process with stages and transitions.  
**What to look for:** Does the output describe a *process* (with dynamics, stages, transitions) or a *taxonomy* (static list of features)? Does it identify what drives transitions between stages?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | No process described; output defines what a scientific concept is without describing formation |
| 1 | Stages listed but static — described as categories, not as a dynamic process; no transition mechanisms |
| 2 | Process described with 2–3 stages and some mention of transitions, but transitions are vague ("over time, the concept becomes more precise") |
| 3 | Process described with clear stages and at least 2 explicit transition mechanisms; some dynamical language (equilibrium, threshold, phase) |
| 4 | Full process with ≥4 stages, explicit and distinct transition mechanisms, and dynamical language throughout; the description has the character of a dynamical system |
| 5 | Rich dynamical account with phase-transition-like characterization; identifies feedback loops, critical thresholds, and irreversibility; the output reads like a physicist describing meaning formation and reveals genuinely non-obvious dynamics |

## Scoring Protocol for TES

1. Score M1–M5 first (following rubric-M1-M5.md protocol).
2. Score TES after M1–M5, on targeted tasks only.
3. Use the task-specific anchor tables above.
4. Record TES score and 1–2 sentence justification.
5. Same blinding and inter-rater reliability requirements as M1–M5 apply.
